Why Hybrid Cueing: The Six-Dimension Analysis

A practitioner's hand does six things. No single actuator covers all six. This is why we run two types.

Bottom line: Vibration covers 2/6 dimensions well (location, timing). Pressure covers 4/6 (magnitude, duration, release, partial direction). Neither alone replicates a trainer's hand. The MVP tests both to find what matters most in practice.

Three-State Cueing Gradient

From practitioner behavior — not binary on/off, but a progression

1. Correction

Strong signal — "you're off, shift activation here." Actuator: Vibration burst (fast, salient attention grab) + TSA squeeze onset (sustained directional pressure).

2. Reinforcement

Light sustained signal — "stay here, you've got it." Actuator: TSA at low squeeze (Merkel/Ruffini stay active). Vibration would habituate here — useless for sustained reinforcement.

3. Fade

Signal off — "pattern ingrained, test independence." Actuator: Gradual TSA release. Abrupt release = "did you maintain without the cue?" This is the motor learning transfer test.

Skin Spatial Resolution — The Binding Constraint

The skin can't resolve stimuli as finely as muscles are compartmentalized. Design to the skin's limits, not the muscle's. The torso has the worst spatial resolution of our target regions (40–60mm TPD) — this directly limits how many independent cue zones we can place per muscle group.

Torso motor unit physiology: For torso muscles, between-muscle distinction is the primary target — pec vs anterior delt, lat vs lower trap, upper trap vs levator scapulae. These are large muscles with distinct bellies spaced well apart, making them well-suited for the torso's coarser spatial resolution. Within-muscle regional cueing (e.g., upper vs lower pec head) is borderline at 40-50mm TPD — achievable for larger muscles like pecs and lats, unreliable for compact muscles like rhomboids. The vest's advantage: torso has many more muscle groups to distinguish between, even if each group only supports 2–3 cue zones.
Deshpande torso-specific finding: Vibrotactile localization was MORE accurate (60.7%) than force localization (54.6%) specifically studied on the torso (Deshpande 2021). This is directly relevant to our vest design — vibration is the better modality for spatial "where" cueing on the torso, while pressure (TSA) is better for sustained "how much / how long" cueing. The hybrid approach is especially justified on the torso where spatial discrimination is worst: vibration says WHERE to activate, pressure says HOW MUCH to engage and FOR HOW LONG to hold. *[Accessed 2026-04-07]*
Dual-modality architecture: Each cue zone on the vest supports both a vibrotactile motor and a TSA contact pad, wired in parallel. The app selects which modality fires per cue event — vibration burst for spatial attention, TSA squeeze for sustained reinforcement, or both for correction. This lets us A/B test modality effectiveness per muscle group without hardware changes. For MVP, both are wired at 2–4 key zones; the user/app toggles which is active. Post-MVP, standardized pod connectors (4-pin: power, GND, SDA, SCL) enable physically swappable vibe-only, squeeze-only, or hybrid pods.

Torso Placement: Front (Chest + Anterior Delts)

Electrode + actuator placement for anterior torso muscle groups. Torso TPD = 40–60mm — between-muscle distinction is primary target, not within-muscle.

sEMG TSA Vibe Chest — Front View

Front torso — 8-10 channels: 2 electrodes per pec (upper/lower or sternal/clavicular head — achievable at 40-50mm TPD), 1 per anterior delt, optional obliques. TSA motors mounted on lateral strap with short routes (<15cm) to pec contact pads. Vibes co-located at each muscle group. Key advantage: pec vs delt distinction is a large between-muscle signal — easy to detect. Demo exercises: bench press, push-up, chest fly.

sEMG TSA Vibe Back — Posterior View

Posterior torso — 10-12 channels: 2 per lat (upper/lower — large muscle belly, achievable at 40-55mm TPD), 1-2 per trap (upper vs mid), 1 per posterior delt, optional lower trap/rhomboid. TSA motors on lateral straps with short routes to mid-back contact pads. Key challenge: scapular movement shifts electrode position during rows/pulldowns — straps anchored to shoulder straps reduce this. Demo exercises: row, pulldown, face pull.

Full Vest: Combined Front + Back

sEMG TSA Vibe Strap-Harness Architecture + Signal Flow

Strap-harness design: Adjustable straps at chest, ribcage, and waist levels with shoulder Y-harness. Pods mount on straps at muscle zones via snap/velcro. CV compatibility: bare skin visible between straps — MediaPipe can track shoulder, elbow, hip keypoints without occlusion. Adjustable buckles accommodate wide range of body sizes without garment sizing. Dual-modality per zone: each pod position supports both vibe motor and TSA contact pad, software selects which fires per cue event. Distributed motors: TSA motors sit on straps near target muscle (<15-20cm route), avoiding the 30-40cm friction limit. One motor per squeeze zone. BLE data rate: 14ch x 500Hz x 2 bytes = 14 kB/s — within BLE 4.2 practical throughput (20-30 kB/s) but uses ~50-70%. Consider batched transmission at 200Hz envelope for reliability.

Modality Selection Per Zone

Software-switchable cueing: Both a vibrotactile motor and a TSA contact pad are wired at each primary zone. The app decides per cue event: Correction = vibe burst (spatial attention) + TSA onset (sustained pressure). Reinforcement = TSA only at low intensity (slow-adapting receptors maintain signal). Fade = gradual TSA release. This lets us A/B test "vibration-only vs pressure-only vs hybrid" per muscle group per user — the key experiment to run during MVP. Post-MVP, standardized 4-pin pod connectors enable physically swappable vibe-only / squeeze-only / hybrid pods.

Torso Vest / Shirt Form Factors

Converting from single-arm compression sleeve to torso garment. Target muscle groups: pecs, lats, traps, delts, upper back, core. Key constraints: TSA string routing on torso can exceed 30-40cm practical max — distributed motors required. CV needs bare skin visible for pose estimation — minimal-coverage harness preferred over full shirt.

Key design tension: sEMG needs skin contact via compression. CV needs bare skin visible. A full compression shirt gives best electrode contact but occludes the torso from the camera. An adjustable strap-harness (like a climbing harness) exposes maximum skin for CV while providing localized compression at each pod zone. Recommended MVP: strap-harness with adjustable buckles at chest, ribcage, and waist levels + shoulder Y-harness. Adjustable buckles also solve the body-sizing problem — one harness fits a wide range of torso sizes vs needing XS-XXL garment SKUs.

A. Full Compression Shirt (Athos-Style)

Pros: Best electrode contact via compression. Single garment covers all torso groups. Professional appearance. Textile electrodes woven at fixed positions (Athos: ~14 channels for pecs, delts, lats, traps, biceps, triceps).
Cons: Fixed electrode positions — can't adjust for anatomy. Size-specific (XS–XXL range needed). Harder to iterate. Not washable with electronics attached.
Best for: Post-MVP polished product. Base: ~$20–30 off-the-shelf compression shirt

B. Modular Vest + Snap-Grid Pods

Pros: Maximum modularity. Populate only the zones you need per exercise. Pods relocatable. Electronics fully removable for washing. Conductive snap buttons at grid points connect electrode pads through fabric windows to skin.
Cons: Vest provides less compression than shirt — may need supplemental adhesive electrodes for consistent contact. More complex wiring harness.
Best for: Development iteration, A/B testing muscle group configurations. Recommended for MVP.

C. Hybrid Shirt + Distributed Motor Pods

Pros: Compression shirt for electrode contact + velcro-mounted motor pods on exterior. Short string routes (motor near target muscle, <20cm) avoid friction budget issues. Motors removable for washing.
Cons: External pods visible on shirt. Heavier than pure sensing.
Best for: Post-MVP when TSA squeeze is validated and needs to feel polished.

D. Electrode Skin Contact Detail

sEMG requires direct skin contact. Two approaches: (1) MVP: User applies adhesive Ag/AgCl electrode on skin, puts on shirt, pod snaps onto shirt over electrode via conductive snap stud. (2) Post-MVP: Pod has integrated dry electrode that protrudes through a ~25mm fabric window, held against skin by compression. Either way, modularity and skin contact are compatible.

Open Questions for Hardware Specialist

Questions from prior sessions (Q1–Q5) updated for torso vest context, plus new torso-specific questions (Q6–Q12).

Q1 — TSA on Torso: Distributed Motors vs Central Hub
How should we place TSA motors on a torso vest where routing distances exceed the 30-40cm practical limit?
On an arm sleeve, a single wrist hub with 25cm string routes worked. On a torso, pec-to-spine is 40-50cm with curves — too much friction loss (>50%). Our proposal: distributed motors on lateral straps with short routes (<15-20cm) to nearby contact pads. One N20 motor per squeeze zone. Trade-off: more motors = more weight, wiring, cost. But each route stays within friction budget. Is this the right call, or is there a clever central-hub solution we're missing?
Q2 — Electrode Contact on Curved Torso Surfaces
Flat Ag/AgCl electrodes on ribcage, scapula, and curved pec surfaces — how much does contact quality degrade?
Arms are roughly cylindrical — electrodes lie flat. The torso has concave (between scapulae), convex (pecs, ribcage), and mixed surfaces. Adhesive Ag/AgCl pads conform somewhat, but large curvature may cause edge lift. Options: (a) Standard flat electrodes + extra adhesive, (b) pre-curved flexible electrodes, (c) smaller electrodes that conform better, (d) compression from harness strap provides contact force. Which approach works best for budget EMG on the torso?
Q3 — Calibration at Scale
The staircase auto-calibration algorithm works for 2 channels. How does it scale to 8-14?
Original algorithm: ramp TSA squeeze, detect EMG RMS change >10-15% above baseline. Converges in 10-20 steps, ~200-500ms per step. For 8+ channels, running sequentially takes 30-60 seconds. Running in parallel risks inter-channel artifact. Question: Can we calibrate all channels simultaneously if squeeze zones are spatially separated? Or must we serialize? What's the user experience if calibration takes 60+ seconds?
Q4 — String Material & Fatigue (unchanged)
Spectra vs Dyneema braid for torso routing — longer runs, more bends.
Same question as before but with torso context: routes are shorter (15-20cm) but may have tighter bends around strap-to-body transitions. Dyneema SK78 (>1000 cycles) is recommended. Spectra braided (~50 cycles) may suffice for MVP testing if we're iterating fast. Is pre-stretching necessary for either material? Any concerns about string routing through adjustable buckle points?
Q5 — Latency Budget (unchanged)
TSA onset is 150–400ms at budget tier. Is that fast enough for torso exercises?
Bench press rep duration ~2-4s, row ~2-3s. Pre-rep cueing (before the movement starts) only needs 400ms. Mid-rep correction during bench press would need <200ms to be useful. For MVP: pre-rep and inter-rep cueing only? Or do we need mid-rep capability for exercises like rows where form breaks down during the pull?
Q6 — Strap-Harness vs Compression Shirt for Torso
Our current proposal is an adjustable strap-harness (like a climbing harness). Does this provide sufficient electrode contact vs a full compression shirt?
The strap-harness maximizes bare skin exposure for CV tracking and adjusts to many body sizes. But electrode contact depends on strap tension at each pod location — inconsistent if straps loosen during exercise. A compression shirt provides uniform contact but occludes more skin and requires size-specific garments. Hybrid option: strap-harness with local compression patches (small neoprene pads under each electrode zone, held by strap tension). Would this give adequate contact?
Q7 — Garment Sizing for Torso
How many size variants do we need for a torso strap-harness to fit most adult bodies?
Arms have relatively small circumference variance (~25-40cm). Torsos vary much more: chest circumference 80-130cm+, significant body composition variation, breast tissue in women changes chest geometry. Adjustable buckle straps help, but strap length and electrode-to-muscle alignment still vary. Proposal: S/M/L strap sets with 15cm adjustment range per buckle. Is that sufficient, or do we need more granularity?
Q8 — Scapular Movement During Back Exercises
How do we handle electrode shift when the scapula moves during rows, pulldowns, and overhead press?
Mid-trap and rhomboid electrodes sit over the scapula region. During protraction/retraction (rowing movements), the scapula slides 5-10cm laterally. Electrodes on a strap-harness may not track this movement — the strap stays put while the scapula slides underneath. This could produce motion artifact or lose contact. Options: (a) Accept the limitation and interpret signal cautiously, (b) mount electrodes on shoulder strap (moves with scapula), (c) use adhesive electrodes at those positions instead of strap-mounted pods.
Q9 — Washability & Sweat Management
Torso generates significantly more sweat than arms. How does this affect electrode signal and harness durability?
Sweat actually improves Ag/AgCl electrode conductivity (temporarily), but excess moisture can bridge between electrode pairs and create short circuits or crosstalk. Nylon/polyester straps absorb less sweat than cotton but still get saturated. Questions: (1) Do we need hydrophobic treatment on strap fabric near electrodes? (2) How often do disposable electrodes need replacement during a sweaty session? (3) Electronics in pods — conformal coating or potting for sweat protection?
Q10 — MVP Exercise Selection for Torso
Which torso exercise is the best MVP demo? Bench press, barbell row, or overhead press?
Bench press: Universally known. "Your right pec is lazy" narrative is relatable. Supine position = less electrode shift. But requires a bench.
Barbell row: "Your traps are compensating for your lats" is a strong narrative. Standing = more electrode shift. Requires barbell.
Push-up: Zero equipment. Can be done anywhere (demo floor). Pec + delt + core activation all measurable. Less load = subtler signal differences. Question for specialist: Which exercise gives cleanest multi-channel torso EMG signal (least motion artifact, most distinct between-muscle activation patterns)?
Q11 — 8+ Channel Analog Multiplexing
Can the ESP32-S3 ADC handle 8-14 channels of sEMG via CD4051 multiplexers at adequate sampling rates?
ESP32-S3 ADC is 12-bit, up to ~20kSPS. With 8:1 mux, each channel gets sampled at 20k/8 = 2500 Hz — sufficient for sEMG (needs >1000 Hz for full spectrum, ~500 Hz for envelope). At 14 channels via two muxes, rate drops to ~1400 Hz/channel — still adequate for envelope but marginal for spectral analysis. Question: Is ESP32 ADC noise floor acceptable for sEMG (<10μV needed), or do we need external ADCs (ADS1292R) even at MVP to get usable signal on the torso where muscle-to-electrode distance is greater?
Q12 — Dual-Modality Pod Wiring
Wiring both a vibe motor and TSA motor at each zone — how complex is the harness wiring?
Each dual-modality zone needs: 2 wires for sEMG (differential pair), 2 wires for vibe motor (PWM + GND), 2 wires for TSA motor (H-bridge pair), plus shared power rail. That's 6-7 wires per zone, 8 zones = 48-56 wires routed through the harness. Options: (a) Per-pod local driver PCB with I2C daisy chain (only 4 wires to each pod: VCC, GND, SDA, SCL), (b) central wiring harness with ribbon cable along straps, (c) start with vibe-only at most zones and TSA only at 2 zones to reduce wiring.
